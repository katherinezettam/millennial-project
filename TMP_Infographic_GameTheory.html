4 principles from game theory that explain why some businesses succeed — and why others collapse doing everything "right"
The Millennial Project

Game theory is not just for economists. It's a map of the invisible dynamics shaping every business decision you make — whether you know the rules or not.
Four principles
⚔️
Principle 01
The Prisoner's Dilemma
When two competitors each act in their own short-term interest, both end up worse off than if they had cooperated. It looks rational. It destroys value.
Real world: In 1992, American Airlines launched a 50% price cut. Every major carrier matched within 24 hours. The industry logged record losses of $1.53 billion — while selling more seats than ever. Every airline made the "logical" move. Every airline lost.
⚖️
Principle 02
Nash Equilibrium
A situation where no one has any reason to change their strategy — given what everyone else is doing. It looks stable. It is often a trap.
Real world: Two coffee shops on the same street both run loyalty programs, both advertise constantly, both launch seasonal drinks. Neither can stop while the other continues. The equilibrium is stable. The equilibrium is also exhausting. The way out: change the game, not your tactics.
📡
Principle 03
Signaling Theory
Customers can't directly measure quality before they buy. So they read signals — your pricing, your branding, your client list. Perception is not vanity. It is information.
Real world: Nobel laureate Michael Spence proved in 1973 that a degree's market value comes not just from knowledge gained, but from the signal of effort it sends. In business: a designer charging €200/hr often wins over one charging €50 — even if their work is identical. The price is the signal.
🔁
Principle 04
Repeated Games & Reputation
When you play the same game with the same people over time, the rules change. Short-term defection becomes expensive. Reputation becomes your most valuable asset.
Real world: Robert Axelrod's 1984 computer tournaments found the winning strategy was simple: cooperate first, retaliate once if crossed, then forgive. Not aggressive. Not a pushover. Players who over-delivered early and protected their reputation consistently outperformed everyone else.

Which game are you in?
Recognise your situation
Most business decisions fail not because the strategy was wrong — but because it was the wrong strategy for the game being played.
Danger zone
Competing on price
You're probably in a prisoner's dilemma. Both sides lose. Exit through differentiation, not better tactics.
Opportunity
Building relationships
You're in a repeated game. Reputation compounds. Over-deliver early and play the long game.
Watch your signals
Entering a new market
Customers can't judge quality yet. Your price, look, and client list are doing the talking before you do.
Equilibrium trap
Copying competitors
You're in a Nash equilibrium. Nobody wins by playing harder. Find a different game to play entirely.
"The businesses that last aren't necessarily the ones who worked hardest. They're the ones who recognised which game they were in — and chose their moves accordingly."

Key takeaways
What to do with this
Stop competing on price If you're in a race to the bottom, exit the race. Change the frame of the competition.
Audit your signals Your pricing, branding and client list send a message before you say a word. Make sure it's the right one.
Play for the long game In most industries, you'll see the same people again. Every interaction is one move in a longer match.
Name the equilibrium If you're stuck doing what everyone else does — and it isn't working — that's not a tactics problem. That's a game problem.
